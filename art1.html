The Sumarian Game

The game:

The Sumerian Game, written and created in 1964, is known to be the first educational video game. Made for the classroom, the device students used to play it was an IBM 1050 terminal, whose output was printed out on paper and the input was through a keyboard. These devices connect to an IBM 7090 which is a computer and projector duo which presented a slide show cut scene for the game. The gameplay has the player being a ruler of a kingdom wherein they must make inventory decisions to feed and care for their people. The basic game mechanics are reading information about resources and a question (e.g. “how many bushels of grain do you want PLANTED for the next crop?”) from the printer then inputting decisions into the keyboard and finally, receiving the outcome of the decision (in printed form).

Educational Value

The main educational goals of this game was to teach students of the Sumer empire, how to rule and resource manage, and how to make sound decisions based on the information they are given.

The Legacy

This game is a text based, resource management game. It was not the first resource management game however it was groundbreaking for being created specifically for classrooms. The Sumerian Game is renowned because of its author, a teacher named Mabel Addis who, through this game, became one of the first female game developers. Furthermore, The Sumerian Game influenced later games such as Hammurabi. Despite this game having no complete copies anymore, this game has a legacy as being one of the first educational games and being created by one of the first female game developers.
